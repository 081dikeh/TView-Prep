Introduce Yourself
Almost always the first question — it sets the entire tone
INTRO "Tell me about yourself" / "Walk me through your background as a frontend developer." +

This question is asked in virtually every interview and it sets the tone for everything that follows. A weak intro makes you sound unconfident. A strong intro signals clarity, seniority, and professional self-awareness.

The Formula: Present → Past → Future

Don't recite your CV. Instead, tell a tight, compelling narrative in this order:

  1. Who you are now — your current role, level, and main area of expertise
  2. What you've built / done — 1–2 concrete, impressive highlights (not a list of every job)
  3. What you're looking for next — why you're here and what excites you about this opportunity

Keep it to 60–90 seconds. Don't ramble. End by inviting the next question.

Strong Template Answer

"I'm a frontend developer with [X] years of experience, specialising in React and building performant, accessible user interfaces. Most recently, I worked at [Company / on Project], where I [specific achievement — e.g., 'rebuilt the dashboard from a legacy jQuery codebase into a React component library, which cut page load time by 40% and reduced bug reports by half']. Before that, I focused on [briefly mention relevant experience]. I enjoy the craft of taking complex requirements and turning them into clean, user-centred products — and I'm particularly interested in working with micro1 because of the opportunity to work on high-calibre remote projects with an international team. I'm excited to show what I can bring."

What to Avoid

  • Don't read your CV chronologically. "I started in 2018 at XYZ, then in 2020 I moved to…" — this is boring and wastes your 60 seconds.
  • Don't be vague. "I work with various web technologies" — says nothing. Be specific.
  • Don't skip the "why here" part. Zara or subsequent questions will probe your motivation.
  • Don't mention salary, remote preference, or personal logistics in the intro.

Tailoring for micro1 Specifically

micro1 hires for global clients across many industries. Mention that you're comfortable working independently, across time zones, and that you take ownership of your work end-to-end. This aligns with what remote-first companies value most.

Pro tip: Practise your intro out loud 3–5 times before the interview. It should feel natural, not rehearsed. Zara will often use something you say in your intro to ask the next question — so mention things you're actually confident going deep on.

Behavioural & Scenario Questions
Zara evaluates how you think, communicate, and problem-solve under ambiguity
B01 Why are you applying to micro1? What motivates you about this kind of role? +

This question tests whether you've done your homework and whether you have real motivation beyond "I need a remote job." Zara is looking for enthusiasm and professional alignment.

What micro1 is looking for

micro1 is a platform that certifies elite remote engineers and matches them with top-tier global clients. They want people who are serious about craft, comfortable with autonomy, and can represent themselves professionally to international companies.

Template Answer

"I'm drawn to micro1 because of the calibre of work and clients available through the platform. I've reached a point in my career where I want to work on problems that genuinely challenge me, and the opportunity to be matched with high-quality global teams is exciting. I also value the remote-first structure — I work well independently and I'm used to taking full ownership of deliverables without needing a lot of hand-holding. Frontend development is where I genuinely love to be — there's a satisfaction in making something performant, clean, and actually usable. micro1's rigorous certification process also signals that the engineers on the platform take their work seriously, which is the kind of environment I thrive in."
Avoid: "I want a remote job so I can travel" or "The pay is good." These come across as transactional, not professional.
B02 Tell me about a project where communication broke down. What happened and how did you handle it? +

This question tests your self-awareness, your ability to work on a team, and whether you take ownership when things go wrong — or blame others.

The STAR Formula

  • Situation — Set the scene briefly
  • Task — What were you trying to accomplish?
  • Action — What did YOU specifically do to address it?
  • Result — What changed? What did you learn?

Template Answer

"On a project to build a new feature, my team and the backend team were building simultaneously with no shared API contract defined upfront. When integration time came, our data shapes didn't match — the backend was returning nested objects where we expected flat arrays. It caused two days of unplanned rework on both sides and delayed the release. I took it as a process failure, not a people failure. I proposed introducing a contract-first approach using OpenAPI specs — before any code is written, both teams agree on the shape of the data. I also set up a shared Slack channel specifically for API design discussions. On the next feature, integration took two hours instead of two days. The lesson I took was: misaligned assumptions are the most expensive kind of bug."
Key: Own your part in the breakdown. Don't say "they didn't communicate well." Say "we didn't have a process that ensured alignment." This shows maturity.
B03 Describe a time you had to make a difficult technical decision with limited information. +

This tests your decision-making process and whether you can act under uncertainty — a critical skill for autonomous remote work.

Template Answer

"We needed to choose a rendering strategy for a new product that had both a public-facing marketing site with SEO requirements and a complex authenticated dashboard. There was pressure to ship quickly and no clear answer on which framework to use. We had 48 hours to decide. I built minimal prototypes in Next.js and a pure React SPA, mapped each approach's trade-offs against our specific requirements, and ran a short async discussion with the team. We chose Next.js — SSG for the public pages, client-only rendering for the dashboard. Within two months, our organic search ranking for key terms improved noticeably, and the dashboard performance was unaffected. The main thing I learned is that a small time investment in evaluation always pays back more than committing to an assumption."
B04 How do you handle working across time zones or with limited real-time collaboration? +

micro1 places engineers with global clients. This question directly tests your fitness for async, remote-first work. The right answer signals ownership and clear communication habits.

Template Answer

"I've worked in async environments before and I've found that the key is over-communicating proactively — especially on blockers. I write clear, thorough async updates so my teammates in different time zones have full context when they come online. I structure my work to front-load anything that needs another person's input so I'm not blocked waiting for a response. I also keep documentation current — decisions, API contracts, component usage — so anyone can get context without asking me directly. I find I'm actually more productive in async environments because I have long uninterrupted blocks to do deep work."

HTML & CSS Questions
Scalability, architecture, cross-browser, layout
Q01 What best practices should be followed for scalable and maintainable HTML/CSS codebases? +

This is one of micro1's listed top questions. They want to hear that you think about CSS as an architecture problem, not just a styling tool.

Semantic HTML

Use elements that describe what content is, not just how it looks. Use <article>, <section>, <nav>, <header>, <main>, <aside>. This improves accessibility for screen readers, improves SEO, and makes your HTML self-documenting.

CSS Architecture — BEM

BEM (Block Element Modifier) is a naming convention that prevents class name collisions and makes styles predictable. The pattern is: .block__element--modifier.

/* Block: a standalone component */
.card { ... }

/* Element: a part of the block */
.card__title { ... }
.card__image { ... }

/* Modifier: a variant of the block or element */
.card--featured { ... }
.card__title--large { ... }

Preprocessors (SASS/SCSS)

Use SCSS for variables ($primary-color), mixins (reusable chunks of CSS), nesting (keep related styles together), and partials (_buttons.scss, _forms.scss) that get compiled into one file.

Minimise Specificity

High-specificity selectors (like #id .class div span) are hard to override and cause "specificity wars" where developers resort to !important. Keep selectors flat — ideally just one class. The lower your specificity, the easier your CSS is to maintain.

Rule of thumb: If you're writing !important, that's a signal of a specificity problem elsewhere — fix the architecture, not the symptom.
Q02 How do you ensure cross-browser compatibility in complex front-end projects? +

Cross-browser issues often come from CSS properties with inconsistent support, JS APIs that don't exist in older browsers, or default styling differences between browsers.

Strategy 1 — Feature Detection over Browser Detection

Never check which browser someone is on — check whether a feature is supported. Use the CSS @supports rule, or the JS library Modernizr.

/* CSS feature query */
@supports (display: grid) {
  .layout { display: grid; }
}
/* Fallback for non-grid browsers */
.layout { display: flex; }

Strategy 2 — Normalize, Don't Reset

Browsers apply their own default styles. normalize.css irons out inconsistencies while preserving useful defaults (unlike a hard reset which zeroes everything out).

Strategy 3 — Polyfills

A polyfill is JavaScript code that provides modern browser functionality to older browsers. For example, fetch, IntersectionObserver, and Promise all needed polyfills for IE support. Tools like Babel transpile modern JS syntax to ES5 automatically.

Strategy 4 — Autoprefixer

CSS vendor prefixes (-webkit-, -moz-) are needed for some properties in older browsers. Use Autoprefixer (a PostCSS plugin) to add these automatically based on a browserslist config — you write clean CSS and the tool handles prefixing.

Strategy 5 — Real Device / Browser Testing

Use BrowserStack or Sauce Labs to test on real browsers (especially Safari on iOS, which has its own WebKit quirks). Chrome DevTools' device emulator helps but is not a full replacement.

Q03 Explain the CSS Box Model and how box-sizing works. +

Every HTML element is rendered as a rectangular box. The box model defines how the total size of that box is calculated. Understanding this is critical for layout work.

The Four Layers

  1. Content — the actual text or image
  2. Padding — transparent space inside the border, around the content
  3. Border — a line around the padding
  4. Margin — transparent space outside the border, separating elements

content-box vs border-box

content-box (default)

Width = content only. Padding and border are added on top. A div with width: 200px; padding: 20px; border: 2px solid renders at 244px wide. Surprising!

border-box (recommended)

Width includes padding and border. A div with width: 200px is always 200px wide, no matter how much padding you add. Predictable.

/* The universal best practice */
*, *::before, *::after {
  box-sizing: border-box;
}
Interview tip: If asked "why would an element be wider than expected?" — the first thing to check is the box model and whether border-box is applied.
Q04 When would you use CSS Grid vs Flexbox? +

This is one of the most commonly asked practical CSS questions. The key distinction is dimensionality.

Flexbox — 1 Dimension

Aligns items in a single row or column. Great for: navbars, button groups, centering an element, card rows, form layouts.

Grid — 2 Dimensions

Controls rows and columns simultaneously. Great for: page layouts, dashboards, image galleries, any grid-like structure.

/* Flexbox: horizontal navbar */
.navbar {
  display: flex;
  align-items: center;
  justify-content: space-between;
  gap: 16px;
}

/* Grid: 3-column card layout */
.card-grid {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-columns: repeat(3, 1fr);
  gap: 24px;
}

/* Responsive grid without media queries */
.auto-grid {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-columns: repeat(auto-fill, minmax(250px, 1fr));
  gap: 20px;
}

They're often used together — Grid for the overall page layout, Flexbox for aligning items inside a grid cell.

Q05 What are progressive enhancement and graceful degradation? +

These are two philosophies for handling browser capability differences.

Progressive Enhancement

Start with a baseline that works everywhere (plain HTML, basic CSS). Then layer in advanced features for capable browsers. The foundation is accessible to all users.

Graceful Degradation

Build for modern browsers first, then ensure older browsers still get a functional (if reduced) experience. You build down, not up.

In practice, progressive enhancement is preferred because it's inclusive-first. For example: a form works with plain HTML <button type="submit">, then JavaScript enhances it with async submission and inline validation for modern browsers.

/* @supports lets you apply styles only when a feature exists */
/* Fallback first (works everywhere): */
.layout { display: flex; }

/* Enhancement (for browsers that support grid): */
@supports (display: grid) {
  .layout { display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; }
}

JavaScript Questions
Core concepts, async, closures, the event loop, and ES6+
Q06 Explain closures in JavaScript with a real-world example. +

A closure is a function that remembers the variables from the scope in which it was created, even after that outer scope has finished executing. This is not a quirk — it's a fundamental and intentional feature of JavaScript.

Why it works

In JavaScript, functions create their own scope. When a function is defined inside another function, the inner function has access to the outer function's variables. Even after the outer function returns, those variables aren't garbage-collected — the inner function holds a reference to them. This "closed over" reference is the closure.

function makeCounter() {
  let count = 0; // This variable persists because of the closure

  return {
    increment() { return ++count; },
    decrement() { return --count; },
    value()     { return count; }
  };
}

const counter = makeCounter();
counter.increment(); // 1
counter.increment(); // 2
counter.decrement(); // 1
counter.value();    // 1

// count is private — nobody outside can access it directly
console.log(count); // ReferenceError

Real-world use cases

  • Data encapsulation / private variables — like the counter above
  • Debounce / throttle — the timer ID lives in a closure between calls
  • Memoisation — the cache object lives in the closure of the memoised function
  • Event handlers with captured state — a click handler that knows which item it belongs to
  • Partial application / currying — creating specialised functions from general ones
// Closure for memoisation
function memoize(fn) {
  const cache = {}; // lives in the closure
  return function(...args) {
    const key = JSON.stringify(args);
    if (cache[key] !== undefined) return cache[key];
    cache[key] = fn(...args);
    return cache[key];
  };
}
Q07 How does the JavaScript event loop work? What is the difference between the microtask queue and the macrotask queue? +

JavaScript is single-threaded — it can only do one thing at a time. The event loop is the mechanism that allows it to handle asynchronous operations without blocking.

The four components

  1. Call Stack — where code executes. Functions are pushed on when called, popped off when they return. Only one thing runs here at a time.
  2. Web APIs (Browser/Node) — async operations (setTimeout, fetch, DOM events) are handed off here. When done, their callbacks are queued.
  3. Microtask Queue — holds callbacks from Promise .then()/.catch() and MutationObserver. Processed immediately after each task, before the next macrotask.
  4. Macrotask Queue (Task Queue) — holds callbacks from setTimeout, setInterval, I/O events. Processed one at a time, one per event loop cycle.

Execution order: Synchronous → Microtasks → Macrotasks

console.log('A'); // 1. Sync — runs immediately

setTimeout(() => console.log('B'), 0); // 4. Macrotask — queued after all microtasks

Promise.resolve()
  .then(() => console.log('C'))  // 3. Microtask — runs before setTimeout
  .then(() => console.log('D')); // 3b. Chained microtask

console.log('E'); // 2. Sync — still in the same synchronous block

// Output: A, E, C, D, B
Why does this matter? Understanding this explains why await yields to other microtasks, why you shouldn't block the call stack with heavy computation, and why UI updates can appear delayed when JS is busy.
Q08 Explain async/await vs Promises. When would you use each? +

async/await is syntactic sugar over Promises — under the hood it uses the same Promise machinery, but gives you code that reads synchronously and is much easier to reason about.

Promises — explicit chaining

fetchUser(id)
  .then(user => fetchOrders(user.id))
  .then(orders => renderOrders(orders))
  .catch(err => showError(err))
  .finally(() => hideSpinner());

async/await — sequential reads like sync code

async function loadOrders(id) {
  try {
    const user   = await fetchUser(id);
    const orders = await fetchOrders(user.id);
    renderOrders(orders);
  } catch (err) {
    showError(err);
  } finally {
    hideSpinner();
  }
}

When to prefer Promises directly

Use Promise.all() when you want to run multiple async operations in parallel, not sequentially. Using await in a loop runs them one after the other — very slow for independent operations.

// ❌ Sequential — slow! Each waits for the previous
for (const id of ids) {
  const user = await fetchUser(id); // waits for each
}

// ✅ Parallel — all requests fire at once
const users = await Promise.all(ids.map(id => fetchUser(id)));
Remember: Promise.all fails fast — if any promise rejects, the whole thing rejects. Use Promise.allSettled if you want all results regardless of individual failures.
Q09 What is event delegation and why is it useful? +

Event delegation is a pattern where you attach one event listener on a parent element instead of many listeners on each child. It works because of event bubbling — when you click a child element, the click event bubbles up through its ancestors.

Without delegation (bad at scale)

// Imagine a list with 1000 items — 1000 listeners!
document.querySelectorAll('.list-item').forEach(item => {
  item.addEventListener('click', handleClick);
});

With delegation (one listener, works on dynamic elements)

// One listener on the parent — handles ALL children
document.querySelector('#list').addEventListener('click', (event) => {
  // event.target is the element that was actually clicked
  if (event.target.matches('.list-item')) {
    handleClick(event.target);
  }
  // .closest() is useful for nested elements
  const item = event.target.closest('.list-item');
  if (item) handleClick(item);
});

Three benefits

  1. Performance — 1 listener vs thousands = less memory usage
  2. Dynamic elements — elements added to the DOM after page load are automatically handled
  3. Less cleanup — you don't need to remove listeners from each element when they're removed

React & State Management
Hooks, Virtual DOM, Context, Redux, data flow patterns
Q10 Explain React's key hooks: useState, useEffect, useMemo, useCallback, useRef. +

useState — reactive local state

Declares a piece of state. When it changes, React re-renders the component. The setter function is asynchronous — the new value won't be available on the next line, only on the next render.

const [count, setCount] = useState(0);
// Use functional update when new state depends on previous state:
setCount(prev => prev + 1); // safe in concurrent mode

useEffect — side effects after render

Runs after the component renders. Used for data fetching, subscriptions, timers, and DOM manipulation. The dependency array controls when it runs: empty array = run once on mount, values in array = run when they change, no array = run after every render (usually wrong).

useEffect(() => {
  const timer = setInterval(() => tick(), 1000);
  return () => clearInterval(timer); // cleanup — runs before next effect or unmount
}, []); // empty array = only on mount/unmount

useMemo — memoised computed value

Caches the result of an expensive calculation. Only recomputes when its dependencies change. Use when you have heavy computation that shouldn't run on every render.

const sortedItems = useMemo(() =>
  [...items].sort((a, b) => a.price - b.price),
  [items] // only re-sorts when items array changes
);

useCallback — memoised function reference

Returns the same function reference between renders if dependencies haven't changed. Critical when passing callbacks to child components wrapped in React.memo — without it, a new function reference is created each render, breaking memoisation.

const handleDelete = useCallback((id) => {
  setItems(prev => prev.filter(item => item.id !== id));
}, []); // stable reference — child won't re-render unnecessarily

useRef — mutable value without re-render

Two use cases: (1) accessing a DOM element directly, (2) storing a mutable value that doesn't trigger a re-render when it changes (like a timer ID or a previous value).

const inputRef = useRef(null);
// Access DOM:
inputRef.current.focus();

// Store mutable value without triggering re-render:
const timerRef = useRef(null);
timerRef.current = setInterval(tick, 1000);
Q11 How do you manage state in large React applications? +

State management is a spectrum. The right solution depends on the scope and complexity of the state, not on what's trendy.

The Scale

  1. useState — for local, single-component state (form inputs, open/closed toggles)
  2. useReducer — for complex local state with multiple sub-values or state transitions (a form with many fields, a multi-step wizard)
  3. Context API — for state that multiple components across the tree need (theme, current user, language). Good for state that changes infrequently — frequent updates cause all consumers to re-render.
  4. Zustand / Jotai / Recoil — lightweight external stores that avoid Context's re-render problem. Excellent for medium-complexity apps.
  5. Redux Toolkit — centralised, predictable state for large apps with complex logic, middleware (e.g. caching, logging), and many contributors who need consistent patterns.

Key principles regardless of tool

  • Never mutate state directly — always return a new object/array
  • Derive, don't duplicate — if you can compute a value from existing state, don't store it separately
  • Lift state up — state should live in the lowest common ancestor that needs it
Interview answer structure: "I start with useState. When prop drilling goes more than 2 levels, I reach for Context or a lightweight store like Zustand. Redux is reserved for apps where the state logic is genuinely complex and shared across many teams or features."
Q12 What is the Virtual DOM and how does React's reconciliation algorithm work? +

Directly manipulating the real DOM is expensive — reading from it forces layout recalculations, and writing to it triggers repaints. React's Virtual DOM solves this.

How it works

  1. React keeps an in-memory tree (Virtual DOM) that mirrors the real DOM
  2. When state or props change, React creates a new Virtual DOM tree
  3. The diffing algorithm compares old vs new trees to find the minimum set of changes
  4. Only those changes are applied to the real DOM in one batch

The diffing algorithm's rules

  • Two elements of different types produce completely different trees — React destroys and rebuilds
  • Elements of the same type are updated in place — only changed attributes are applied
  • The key prop on list items tells React which items are which across renders, enabling accurate diffing without reordering
// Without key — React can't tell what changed, may re-render all
{items.map(item => <Item data={item} />)}

// With key — React efficiently tracks each item
{items.map(item => <Item key={item.id} data={item} />)}
Don't use array index as key if the list can be reordered or filtered — index-based keys cause bugs because the key no longer uniquely identifies the same item across renders. Always use a stable unique ID.

Performance Optimisation
Images, bundles, rendering, Core Web Vitals
Q13 What are your strategies for front-end web performance optimisation? +

Performance optimisation is a broad topic. The best answers are structured around measuring first, then applying targeted solutions. Don't optimise blindly.

Measure First

Use Chrome Lighthouse (built into DevTools) to get a performance score and identify specific issues. Track Core Web Vitals: LCP (Largest Contentful Paint, should be <2.5s), FID/INP (interaction delay), and CLS (layout shift).

JavaScript

  • Code splitting — split your JS bundle by route using dynamic import(). Users only download the code for the page they're on.
  • Tree shaking — bundlers (Webpack, Vite) automatically remove unused exports. Write side-effect-free code to enable this.
  • Defer non-critical JS — <script defer> parses HTML without blocking; <script async> loads and executes without blocking parsing.

Images

  • Use WebP or AVIF format — typically 25-50% smaller than JPEG/PNG with same quality
  • Use srcset to serve correct sizes for different screen widths
  • Add loading="lazy" to below-the-fold images

Network

  • CDN — serve static assets from edge nodes geographically close to users
  • Browser caching — use long-lived Cache-Control headers + content hashing in filenames
  • HTTP/2 — multiplexes multiple requests over one connection; reduces connection overhead

Rendering

  • Virtualise long lists — only render visible items (react-window, react-virtual)
  • Avoid layout thrashing — don't read and write to the DOM in alternating loops
  • Use CSS animations over JS animations — CSS runs on the compositor thread and doesn't block the main thread

Responsive Design
Layouts, breakpoints, units, accessibility
Q14 What are the main considerations when building responsive layouts? +

Mobile-First Approach

Write base styles for the smallest screen, then use min-width media queries to scale up. This ensures you're layering complexity, not fighting it — and mobile users get the smallest CSS payload.

/* Base: mobile */
.grid { display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr; }

/* Tablet */
@media (min-width: 768px) {
  .grid { grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; }
}

/* Desktop */
@media (min-width: 1200px) {
  .grid { grid-template-columns: repeat(3, 1fr); }
}

Relative Units

  • rem — relative to the root font size (16px default). Great for font sizes, margins, padding.
  • em — relative to the element's font size. Useful for component-local scaling.
  • %, vw, vh — relative to parent or viewport. Great for widths and heights.
  • Avoid px for font sizes — it ignores user browser font preferences, hurting accessibility.

Touch Targets

Interactive elements should be at least 44×44px (Apple's guideline) so they're easy to tap on mobile. Buttons that are 28px tall with 8px padding around them fail this threshold.

Content-First Breakpoints

Don't choose breakpoints based on device sizes (768px, 1024px). Choose them based on where your content breaks — where the layout looks wrong. This makes your design more robust.

Tooling & Debugging
DevTools, testing, linting, version control
Q15 What techniques are effective for debugging complex front-end issues? +

Chrome DevTools — your main weapon

  • Elements tab — inspect and live-edit the DOM and CSS. Check computed styles to find where a style is coming from.
  • Console tab — logs, errors, warnings. Use console.table() for arrays of objects, console.group() for organised logs.
  • Sources tab — set breakpoints, step through code line by line, watch variable values. The most powerful debugging tool — use it instead of console.log for complex bugs.
  • Network tab — inspect every HTTP request: URL, method, status code, payload, response. Filter by XHR/Fetch to see API calls.
  • Performance tab — record and analyse rendering, scripting, and layout performance. Find long tasks that block the main thread.

React DevTools (browser extension)

Inspect the component tree, view props and state for any component, see why a component re-rendered, and use the Profiler to find unnecessary renders.

Systematic debugging approach

  1. Reproduce reliably — can you make the bug happen consistently? If not, you can't verify a fix.
  2. Isolate — narrow down to the smallest piece of code that exhibits the issue.
  3. Hypothesise and test — form a theory, test it, don't just try random things.
  4. Fix, then verify — after fixing, confirm the bug is gone and you haven't introduced regressions.

Architecture & System Design
SSR, CSR, SSG, Next.js, code structure
Q16 What are the trade-offs between Server-Side Rendering (SSR), Client-Side Rendering (CSR), and Static Site Generation (SSG)? +
CSR — Client-Side Rendering

Server sends a blank HTML shell + JS bundle. Browser runs the JS to build the DOM. Pros: fast navigation after initial load, great for dashboards behind auth. Cons: slow initial load, poor SEO (crawlers may not execute JS).

SSR — Server-Side Rendering

Server generates full HTML per request and sends it. Pros: fast First Contentful Paint, excellent SEO, dynamic per-user content. Cons: higher server load, slower response than cached static files.

SSG — Static Site Generation

HTML generated at build time, served as static files from CDN. Pros: extremely fast (no server processing), great SEO, cheap to host. Cons: content is stale until next build, not suitable for frequently changing data.

ISR — Incremental Static Regeneration

Next.js feature — pages are statically generated but can be regenerated in the background after a set interval. Best of SSG and SSR. Use for: e-commerce product pages, blog posts that update occasionally.

Practical answer: "I'd use SSG for marketing and documentation pages, SSR for pages with personalised or frequently changing data, and CSR for the authenticated dashboard. Next.js lets me use all three in the same app."

EASY 1. Debounce Function
Implement debounce(fn, delay). It should delay invoking fn until after delay ms have elapsed since the last time it was called. Very commonly asked at micro1.
function debounce(fn, delay) {
  // your code here
}

// Usage:
const search = debounce((q) => console.log('Searching:', q), 300);

search('h');   // cancelled
search('he');  // cancelled
search('hel'); // fires after 300ms — only this one
function debounce(fn, delay) {
  let timer; // persists between calls via closure

  return function(...args) {
    clearTimeout(timer);       // cancel any pending call
    timer = setTimeout(() => {
      fn.apply(this, args);     // preserve 'this' context and all arguments
    }, delay);
  };
}

// Real-world usage
const searchInput = document.getElementById('search');
searchInput.addEventListener('input', debounce((e) => {
  fetchResults(e.target.value);
}, 400));

The core insight: The timer variable lives in a closure — it persists across multiple calls to the returned function. Every time the returned function is called, it cancels the previous timer and starts a new one. The original fn only executes if the caller stops calling for delay ms.

Why fn.apply(this, args)? If the debounced function is a method on an object, using apply ensures this refers to the correct object. Using a simple arrow function inside setTimeout captures this from the outer scope.

When to use it: Search inputs (don't fire API calls on every keystroke), window resize handlers, form validation on change.

Debounce vs Throttle: Debounce = fire once after the caller stops. Throttle = fire at most once per time window, regardless of how many calls happen.

EASY 2. Flatten a Nested Array
Write a function to flatten a deeply nested array to a single level without using Array.flat(). You must handle arbitrary nesting depth.
flatten([1, [2, [3, [4, [5]]]]]);
// Expected: [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]

// Solution 1: reduce + recursion (clean, functional)
function flatten(arr) {
  return arr.reduce((acc, item) =>
    acc.concat(Array.isArray(item) ? flatten(item) : item),
  []);
}

// Solution 2: explicit recursion (easier to read aloud)
function flatten(arr) {
  const result = [];
  for (const item of arr) {
    if (Array.isArray(item)) {
      result.push(...flatten(item)); // spread recursive result
    } else {
      result.push(item);
    }
  }
  return result;
}

// What Array.flat() does (for reference):
[1, [2, [3]]].flat(Infinity); // [1, 2, 3]

The core idea: Recursion. For each item in the array: if it's an array, flatten it (recurse); if it's not, add it to results. The recursion naturally handles any depth.

Why not just use flat()? The interviewer wants to see that you understand recursion and can work with arrays programmatically. Always mention "In production I'd use .flat(Infinity), but here's the manual implementation."

Trade-off to mention: Deep recursion on very large arrays can hit the call stack limit. An iterative solution using a stack (array) avoids this.

// Iterative solution using a stack (avoids call stack limits)
function flattenIterative(arr) {
  const stack = [...arr];
  const result = [];
  while (stack.length) {
    const item = stack.pop();
    Array.isArray(item) ? stack.push(...item) : result.unshift(item);
  }
  return result;
}

MEDIUM 4. Custom useFetch Hook
Build a reusable useFetch(url) React hook that manages loading, data, and error states. Handle cleanup to prevent memory leaks when the component unmounts during a request.
// Build this hook:
const { data, loading, error } = useFetch('https://api.example.com/users');

// When the URL changes, it should re-fetch
// It should set loading = true while fetching
// It should handle network errors gracefully
// It should NOT update state if the component unmounted
import { useState, useEffect } from 'react';

function useFetch(url) {
  const [data, setData]       = useState(null);
  const [loading, setLoading] = useState(true);
  const [error, setError]     = useState(null);

  useEffect(() => {
    let cancelled = false; // flag to prevent setState after unmount

    const load = async () => {
      setLoading(true);
      setError(null);
      try {
        const res = await fetch(url);
        if (!res.ok) throw new Error(`Error: ${res.status} ${res.statusText}`);
        const json = await res.json();
        if (!cancelled) setData(json);   // only update if still mounted
      } catch (err) {
        if (!cancelled) setError(err.message);
      } finally {
        if (!cancelled) setLoading(false);
      }
    };

    load();

    return () => { cancelled = true; }; // cleanup: cancel on unmount or url change
  }, [url]); // re-fetch whenever url changes

  return { data, loading, error };
}

// Usage in a component:
function UserList() {
  const { data, loading, error } = useFetch('/api/users');
  if (loading) return <p>Loading...</p>;
  if (error)   return <p>Error: {error}</p>;
  return <ul>{data.map(u => <li key={u.id}>{u.name}</li>)}</ul>;
}

The cancellation pattern: The cancelled boolean flag is the most important part. Here's the problem it solves: if the user navigates away while a fetch is in flight, the component unmounts. When the fetch finishes, it tries to call setData on an unmounted component. React warns about this (memory leak). Setting cancelled = true in the cleanup function prevents the setState calls.

Why not AbortController? AbortController is the modern way to cancel fetch requests entirely (the request is cancelled at the network level, not just ignored). It's better but more complex. Both approaches are valid — mentioning AbortController as an upgrade shows senior knowledge.

// AbortController upgrade (bonus points)
useEffect(() => {
  const controller = new AbortController();
  fetch(url, { signal: controller.signal })
    .then(r => r.json())
    .then(setData)
    .catch(err => { if (err.name !== 'AbortError') setError(err.message); });
  return () => controller.abort(); // actually cancels the network request
}, [url]);

useEffect dependency array: [url] — this makes the effect re-run whenever the URL changes, which is exactly the behaviour we want. The cleanup function runs before the next effect execution, cancelling the previous request.

MEDIUM 6. Throttle Function
Implement throttle(fn, limit) — the function should fire at most once every limit milliseconds, no matter how many times it's invoked.
function throttle(fn, limit) {
  // your code here
}

// If scroll fires 60 times per second, throttle to 10 times per second:
window.addEventListener('scroll', throttle(handleScroll, 100));
function throttle(fn, limit) {
  let lastCallTime = 0; // timestamp of the last execution

  return function(...args) {
    const now = Date.now();
    if (now - lastCallTime >= limit) {
      lastCallTime = now;
      return fn.apply(this, args);
    }
  };
}

// Real usage
window.addEventListener('scroll', throttle((e) => {
  updateScrollPosition(window.scrollY);
}, 100));

Core logic: Record when the function last ran (lastCallTime). On each call, check if enough time has elapsed since then. If yes, run it and update the timestamp. If no, ignore the call.

vs Debounce again:

  • Debounce: "Only run after the caller has been silent for X ms." → good for: search, resize, form save
  • Throttle: "Run at most once per X ms, even if called constantly." → good for: scroll, mousemove, animation frames, button spam

Limitation of this implementation: It drops the last call if it doesn't happen exactly at a boundary. A more robust implementation uses a trailing call (uses setTimeout) to ensure the last invocation always fires. Mentioning this shows depth.

HARD 7. Implement Promise.all from Scratch
Implement myPromiseAll(promises) — it should resolve with an array of all results in order when all promises resolve, or reject immediately if any promise rejects.
myPromiseAll([
  Promise.resolve(1),
  Promise.resolve(2),
  Promise.resolve(3),
]).then(console.log); // [1, 2, 3]

myPromiseAll([
  Promise.resolve(1),
  Promise.reject(new Error('failed')),
]).catch(console.error); // Error: failed
function myPromiseAll(promises) {
  return new Promise((resolve, reject) => {
    // Edge case: empty array resolves immediately
    if (promises.length === 0) return resolve([]);

    const results = new Array(promises.length);
    let completed = 0;

    promises.forEach((promise, index) => {
      // Wrap in Promise.resolve() to handle non-promise values
      Promise.resolve(promise)
        .then(value => {
          results[index] = value; // store at original index to preserve order
          completed++;
          if (completed === promises.length) {
            resolve(results);   // all done!
          }
        })
        .catch(reject);       // any single rejection rejects the whole thing
    });
  });
}

Key insight 1 — counter, not sequential waiting: All promises are started simultaneously. We use a completed counter to track how many have resolved. When the counter reaches the total, we know all are done. This is fundamentally parallel.

Key insight 2 — index-based storage: We store results at results[index], not with push(). This preserves the original input order in the output array, regardless of which promise resolves first. This is the most common mistake people make.

// ❌ Wrong — push order depends on resolution order, not input order
promise.then(value => { results.push(value); });

// ✅ Correct — index preserves input order
promise.then(value => { results[index] = value; });

Key insight 3 — fail fast: .catch(reject) immediately rejects the outer Promise with the first error. The other promises continue running (JavaScript doesn't cancel them), but their results are ignored.

Bonus — what if you want all results regardless? That's Promise.allSettled(). Each result is either { status: 'fulfilled', value } or { status: 'rejected', reason }. Mentioning this shows you know the full Promise API.

HARD 8. Deep Clone an Object
Write a deep clone function that handles nested objects, arrays, Dates, and circular references — without using JSON.parse/stringify.
// JSON.parse/stringify fails for:
// - Functions and undefined (silently removed)
// - Date objects (converted to strings, not Date instances)
// - Circular references (throws an error)
// - Special types like Map, Set, RegExp

const obj = {
  name: 'test',
  date: new Date(),
  nested: { arr: [1, 2, 3] }
};
const clone = deepClone(obj);
clone.nested.arr.push(4);
console.log(obj.nested.arr); // [1, 2, 3] — original unaffected
function deepClone(value, seen = new WeakMap()) {
  // Primitives (string, number, boolean, null, undefined) — return as-is
  if (value === null || typeof value !== 'object') {
    return value;
  }

  // Circular reference check — return previously cloned version
  if (seen.has(value)) return seen.get(value);

  // Handle Date — construct a new Date from the same timestamp
  if (value instanceof Date) return new Date(value.getTime());

  // Handle Array
  if (Array.isArray(value)) {
    const clone = [];
    seen.set(value, clone); // register BEFORE recursing (handles circular)
    value.forEach((item, i) => { clone[i] = deepClone(item, seen); });
    return clone;
  }

  // Handle plain object
  const clone = {};
  seen.set(value, clone); // register BEFORE recursing
  for (const key in value) {
    if (Object.prototype.hasOwnProperty.call(value, key)) {
      clone[key] = deepClone(value[key], seen);
    }
  }
  return clone;
}

Why not JSON.parse/stringify? It's a common shortcut, but it has serious limitations: functions and undefined are silently removed, Date objects become strings, and circular references throw a TypeError. Knowing these limitations is what makes you senior.

The WeakMap for circular references: A circular reference is when an object contains a reference back to itself (obj.self = obj). Without protection, deep cloning this would recurse infinitely and crash. The WeakMap maps original objects to their clones. If we encounter an object we've already started cloning, we return the in-progress clone instead of recursing again.

// Circular reference example
const a = { name: 'a' };
a.self = a; // circular!
deepClone(a); // works correctly with WeakMap protection

Why WeakMap instead of Map? WeakMap keys must be objects (not primitives) and are held weakly — they don't prevent garbage collection. This means our seen map doesn't cause memory leaks by holding references to cloned objects after the function completes.

What this doesn't handle (for honesty): Functions, Symbols, Map, Set, RegExp, ArrayBuffer. Mention these as limitations if asked. For a production-grade solution, you'd use structuredClone() (modern browser API) or a library like Lodash's _.cloneDeep.

// The problem:
// - 10,000 DOM nodes = browser lag / crash
// - Most of those nodes are not visible at any time
//
// The solution: "virtual" rendering
// - Measure scroll position
// - Calculate which items are in the visible window
// - Only render those + a small buffer
// - Use CSS positioning to place them correctly

const items = Array.from({ length: 10000 }, (_, i) => `Item ${i + 1}`);

import { useState } from 'react';

function VirtualList({ items, rowHeight = 40, containerHeight = 400 }) {
  const [scrollTop, setScrollTop] = useState(0);

  const totalHeight  = items.length * rowHeight;

  // Which item index is at the top of the viewport?
  const startIndex   = Math.floor(scrollTop / rowHeight);

  // How many items fit in the viewport? Add 2 as an overscan buffer
  const visibleCount = Math.ceil(containerHeight / rowHeight) + 2;

  // Don't exceed the array bounds
  const endIndex     = Math.min(startIndex + visibleCount, items.length);

  const visibleItems = items.slice(startIndex, endIndex);

  return (
    <div
      style={{ height: containerHeight, overflowY: 'auto', position: 'relative' }}
      onScroll={e => setScrollTop(e.currentTarget.scrollTop)}
    >
      {/* This div gives the scrollbar the correct full height */}
      <div style={{ height: totalHeight, position: 'relative' }}>
        {visibleItems.map((item, i) => {
          const absoluteIndex = startIndex + i;
          return (
            <div
              key={absoluteIndex}
              style={{
                position: 'absolute',
                top: absoluteIndex * rowHeight, // position at correct offset
                height: rowHeight,
                width: '100%',
                display: 'flex',
                alignItems: 'center',
                padding: '0 12px',
                borderBottom: '1px solid #eee',
              }}
            >
              {item}
            </div>
          );
        })}
      </div>
    </div>
  );
}

The core idea in plain English: Instead of rendering 10,000 rows, we render only the ~12 that are visible in the 400px container at any given scroll position. We use position: absolute and top to place them at the correct visual position. A large spacer div (10,000 × 40px = 400,000px tall) gives the scrollbar the correct proportions.

Step-by-step math example: If the user has scrolled 800px down:

  • startIndex = Math.floor(800 / 40) = 20 — start at item 20
  • visibleCount = Math.ceil(400 / 40) + 2 = 12 — render 12 items
  • endIndex = Math.min(20 + 12, 10000) = 32 — end at item 32
  • Item 20 gets top: 20 × 40 = 800px — exactly where the user is scrolled to

The overscan buffer (+2): We render 2 extra items beyond the visible viewport. This prevents a white flash as the user scrolls quickly before the next render kicks in.

Performance consideration: onScroll fires very frequently. In a real app you'd throttle the scroll handler or use useRef to avoid setState on every pixel. Or better yet — use react-window or @tanstack/react-virtual which handle all of this robustly.

What to say in the interview: "In production I'd use react-window or react-virtual — they handle edge cases like variable row heights and overscan much more robustly. But understanding the underlying mechanism — scroll position → index math → absolute positioning — is what makes you able to debug or extend those libraries."
